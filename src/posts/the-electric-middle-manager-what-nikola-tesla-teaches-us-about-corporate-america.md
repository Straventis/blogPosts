### *If you think your job is a thankless grind, meet Nikola Tesla*

I've had bosses who took credit for my work. I've had a bonus disappear into thin air right after I earned it. I've watched someone else get the promotion off an idea I built the deck for. Normal corporate stuff, annoying, forgettable by Friday. Then I read about Nikola Tesla and realized I don't even know what a bad week at work actually looks like.

Born to a priest and a mother who invented her own kitchen tools, including a mechanical eggbeater, Tesla grew up to become one of the greatest electrical minds who's ever lived. And he still died broke, alone, in a hotel room in New York. Being a genius who helped invent the modern electrical grid is, weirdly, a lot like being a mid level PM just trying to get one meeting to end on time.

## The boss who "forgot" your bonus

Everybody's had a bad manager. Yours probably wasn't Thomas Edison. Tesla showed up in America in 1884 with 4 cents to his name, got hired at Edison's company, and was promised $50,000 if he could fix Edison's DC generator design. He did it. Then he asked to get paid. Edison told him, and I'm not exaggerating this quote, that Tesla didn't understand "American humor." Tesla quit that same day and went and dug ditches for $2 a day. Next time your manager "forgets" the raise they promised you in Q2, just know you're in excellent company.

## The deal you don't walk away from, except he did

Tesla licensed his AC motor to George Westinghouse, and together they beat Edison in what's genuinely called the War of the Currents, a real historical event, not a metaphor. Years later, Westinghouse was drowning financially and asked Tesla to let him out of the royalty agreement. Tesla, because he actually trusted the guy, tore up the contract on the spot. That single piece of paper would have made him one of the richest men who ever lived, worth billions today. He just gave it away. I've seen people fight over who gets credit on a Jira ticket. Tesla tore up a path to being a billionaire because a friend asked nicely.

## The budget cut heard around the world

Tesla eventually pitched the ultimate exec sponsor, J.P. Morgan, on wireless power for the entire planet. Morgan bit, handing over $150,000 to get it built. Tesla broke ground on a 186 foot tower out on Long Island, blew through the budget faster than planned, and went back asking for more.

Morgan said no. Worse, he reportedly made sure nobody else in banking would touch the project either. Dead in the water.

Around that same time, an Italian inventor named Marconi sent a radio signal across the Atlantic, using 17 of Tesla's own patents to pull it off. He walked away with the credit and the fortune while Tesla watched from the sidelines. It took until 1943, the same year Tesla died, for the U.S. Supreme Court to finally rule that Tesla, not Marconi, actually invented radio. He won the lawsuit and lost the timeline entirely, vindicated a little too late to enjoy it.

If your VP has ever killed your budget, only to greenlight the exact same idea six months later once another team pitched it, you already know precisely how this felt. Different century, same meeting.

## Burnout, but make it 1930s

By his later years Tesla was in real trouble, and I don't say that as a punchline. He developed genuine obsessive compulsive patterns, only staying in hotel rooms with numbers divisible by three, an intense aversion to touching things like pearls, a mind that by every account never really got to rest. Somewhere in there he also told the press he'd built a "death beam" capable of stopping an army in an instant. Nobody funded it. Nobody believed him anymore. That part isn't funny, it's actually kind of heartbreaking, a man who reshaped how the entire world gets electricity, reduced to being written off as a crank because nobody was checking in on him.

## Legacy, the pigeon

Near the end, Tesla's closest relationship on this earth was with a white pigeon he fed every day at the park. He said he loved her the way a person loves another person. When she died, he said he knew his life's work was done too. He died not long after, in debt, alone, in a hotel room, in January 1943. His belongings eventually made their way to a museum in Belgrade, where you can still go see them today.

So the next time your strategy gets shot down by someone who clearly didn't read past slide two, take a breath. You are not trying to beam electricity through the crust of the earth while your own investor quietly kills your reputation around town. And if it's a really bad day, there's always a park bench and a bag of birdseed. Tesla would understand completely.
